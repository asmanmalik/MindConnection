Trauma & PTSD care

Trauma-informed care for post-traumatic stress.

At Mind and Peace Connection Center, we provide trauma-informed assessment and treatment for individuals living with the effects of difficult or overwhelming experiences. Our clinicians prioritize safety, predictability, and collaboration as we work with you to reduce distress and support recovery.

Understanding trauma and post-traumatic stress

Trauma can refer to experiences that are overwhelming, frightening, or deeply distressing. These events can affect how you feel, how your body responds to stress, and how you see yourself and the world. Some individuals develop post-traumatic stress symptoms that persist long after the event has ended.

Common experiences may include:

  • Intrusive memories or reminders of what happened
  • Feeling “on edge,” easily startled, or constantly alert
  • Avoiding places, conversations, or activities that bring up memories
  • Changes in sleep, mood, or concentration
  • Feeling disconnected from yourself or others
  • Difficulty feeling safe, even in everyday situations

These reactions are understandable responses to very difficult experiences. They are also treatable, and you do not have to navigate them alone.

Our trauma-informed approach

Our goal is to provide care that is respectful, structured, and guided by your pace. Trauma-informed care means that we are attentive to safety, choice, and the impact of past experiences on how you feel in the present.

Safety and stabilization first

We begin by focusing on helping you feel as safe and grounded as possible, both in sessions and in daily life. We discuss what helps you feel more comfortable and what might make sessions feel manageable, and we avoid pushing you to share more than you wish.

Collaboration and choice

You are an active partner in decisions about your care. We explain treatment options in clear language and invite questions and feedback. You can let us know what feels manageable, and we will adjust the pace together.

Evidence-based therapies

We draw on trauma-focused, evidence-based therapies that have been shown to help with post-traumatic stress. We may integrate skills for managing distress, understanding triggers, and building a greater sense of control, based on what is appropriate for you.

Holistic support for daily life

We consider how sleep, stress, relationships, and other aspects of daily life interact with trauma responses. We may suggest practical strategies for building routines, setting boundaries, and identifying supportive resources.

If you have had previous experiences with care that felt too fast or overwhelming, you are welcome to share that with us so we can proceed more carefully.

What to expect in care at Mind and Peace Connection Center

We recognize that beginning trauma-focused care can feel uncertain. Our process is designed to be structured, transparent, and guided by your readiness.

1. Initial consultation and safety planning

In the first visit, we focus on understanding what you are experiencing now, your goals for treatment, and what helps you feel safer. You can share as much or as little detail as you feel comfortable. We may also discuss immediate supports or safety strategies if needed.

2. Developing a trauma-informed treatment plan

If we agree that our services are a good fit, we develop a treatment plan together. This may include trauma-focused therapy, skills for managing distress, and, when appropriate, coordination with psychiatric care. We explain the plan in clear terms and invite your input.

3. Ongoing support and adjustment

As treatment progresses, we regularly review what is helpful and where adjustments might be needed. We recognize that trauma work can be challenging, and we aim to move at a pace that feels tolerable while still supporting progress toward your goals.

You are not expected to share details of what happened before you are ready. Our priority is to build a foundation of safety and trust.

Is trauma and PTSD care right for you?

Individuals seek trauma-focused care for many reasons. You do not need a specific diagnosis of PTSD to ask for help.

You may consider this service if:

  • You notice ongoing changes in mood, sleep, or sense of safety following difficult experiences.
  • You feel affected by past events more than you would like, or in ways that are hard to explain.
  • Certain reminders, places, or situations feel particularly distressing or hard to approach.

It is appropriate to reach out even if:

  • You are unsure whether what you experienced “counts” as trauma.
  • You have mixed feelings about discussing past events.
  • You have had previous treatment and would like to explore a different approach.

Next steps

If you are uncertain whether trauma-focused care is the right next step, you are welcome to contact us to discuss your situation and options. Together, we can consider what type of support may be most appropriate.

Frequently asked questions

Do I have to talk about what happened in detail?

No. We will not ask you to describe events in detail before you are ready. Early sessions often focus on your current symptoms, safety, and coping strategies. Over time, we can discuss how much detail feels manageable and proceed at your pace.

What if I feel worse when I start talking about trauma?

It is common to have concerns about feeling worse when beginning trauma-focused care. We work to establish grounding skills and safety strategies early in treatment and check in regularly about how you are feeling. You can let us know at any time if something feels too intense so we can adjust the approach.

Can this be done through teletherapy?

In some cases, trauma-focused care can be provided through secure online sessions. Together, we will consider whether teletherapy is appropriate and how to set up a space that feels as private and safe as possible.

What if I am already working with another clinician?

If you are already receiving care, we can discuss how our services might complement your existing supports. With your permission, we may coordinate with your other providers to help maintain continuity of care.
